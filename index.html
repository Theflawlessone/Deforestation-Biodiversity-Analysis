Hypothesis & Objectives

States experiencing higher deforestation and rising temperature threats between 2019â€“2021 will also show lower biodiversity, reflected by reduced species richness and higher threatened/endangered species counts.

  • Quantify biodiversity across U.S. states using national park species data.
  • Integrate deforestation and climate data to identify environmental predictors of biodiversity change.
  • Develop and evaluate regression models linking temperature, deforestation, and park acreage to biodiversity outcomes such as plot count, nativeness, and species richness.
  • Assess statistical significance, multicollinearity, model fit, stability, and predictive potential of environmental variables related to TE species counts.

Interpretations

The analyses suggest that short-term deforestation effects on biodiversity may take longer to manifest. Temperature consistently emerged as a stronger immediate predictor of threatened/endangered species counts, while park acreage moderated these risks. Seasonal models indicate that warm-season stress amplifies species vulnerability.
