Deforestation & Biodiversity Analysis

This project investigates forest loss across U.S. states and its relationship with biodiversity and temperature from 2019–2021.

Key Findings

  • Plot_count (a proxy for survey density/fragmentation) was the strongest predictor of deforestation.
  • Species richness and threatened/endangered species counts were weaker predictors in the short-term dataset.
  • Temperature improved model explanatory power (~20%) and was significantly associated with TE species counts (R² ≈ 0.21, p ≈ 0.0327).
  • Seasonal models showed stronger temperature-biodiversity relationships (R² ≈ 0.50–0.54), particularly during summer.

Final Report

Hypothesis & Objectives: States experiencing higher deforestation and rising temperature threats between 2019–2021 will show lower biodiversity.

  • Quantify biodiversity across U.S. states using national park species data.
  • Integrate deforestation and climate data to identify environmental predictors of biodiversity change.
  • Develop and evaluate regression models linking temperature, deforestation, and park acreage to biodiversity outcomes.
  • Assess statistical significance, multicollinearity, model fit, and predictive potential of environmental variables.

Interpretations

Temperature consistently emerged as a stronger predictor of threatened/endangered species counts than deforestation, while park acreage moderated risks. Seasonal models highlight warm-season stress as the most influential.

Conclusions & Next Steps

Temperature is a more immediate driver of biodiversity risk than short-term deforestation. Plot_count reflects survey intensity. Conservation areas provide buffering effects. Future work should expand temporal and spatial coverage, integrate additional environmental stressors, and apply advanced modeling techniques for predictive insights.
